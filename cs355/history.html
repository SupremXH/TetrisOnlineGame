In 1979, Alexey Pajitnov joined the Computer Center of the Soviet Academy of Sciences as a speech recognition researcher. When he was responsible for testing the function of the new hardware, his ambition was to use computers to make people happy. Pajitnov developed several puzzle games on the computer of the institute, namely Electronika 60, which was a scarce resource at the time. For Pajitnov, "Games allow people to understand each other better and act as a revealer of things that people would not normally notice." In 1984, while trying to remake his favorite puzzle game featuring pentose from childhood, Pajitnov thought of a game that consisted of randomly dropped parts that players could fill in rows. Pajitnov believed that the game would become unnecessarily complicated due to changes in twelve different shapes, so he narrowed the concept to four elements, including seven variants. Pajitnov named the game "Tetris", a word composed of "tetromino" and his favorite sport "tennis". Because Electronika 60 does not have a graphical interface, Pajitnov uses spaces and parentheses to model fields and fragments. Realizing that the completed lines quickly filled the screen, Pajitnov decided to delete them, thus creating a key part of the Tetris game. This early version of Tetris had no scoring system and no levels, but its addictive quality distinguished it from other puzzle games created by Pajitnov.
